# Industry Dossier: US Automotive OEM

## Landscape

### Market Size & Volume

### Growth Trajectory

The US auto market hit a post-pandemic recovery peak of 16.2M units in 2025, but 2026 projections signal contraction. Three forces are compressing demand simultaneously: tariff-driven price inflation (average transaction prices up 10%+), federal EV credit expiration, and an emerging affordability crisis pushing buyers toward used vehicles. The January 2026 SAAR of 14.9M is already signaling weakness.

**2024:** Recovery year -- 15.85M units, +2.2% over 2023, best since 2019.
**2025:** Continued growth to 16.2M (+2.4%), but Q2-Q3 saw pull-forward buying ahead of tariff and credit deadlines -- borrowing from future demand.
**2026:** Projected decline to 15.9M (-3%). Tariff hangover, credit expiration, price inflation dampening demand.

### Market Structure

Moderately consolidated. Top 3 OEMs (GM, Toyota, Ford) control ~45% of US sales. Top 5 control ~65%. The long tail is consolidating under cost pressure -- Stellantis declining to historic lows, smaller OEMs capital-constrained. US-built vehicles now represent >55% of sales (up 4pp YoY) as tariffs reshape production geography.

## Trends

### 1. EV Transition: Real but Non-Linear

Global EV sales reached 18M units in 2025 (up from 14M in 2024), but the US remains 82% ICE powertrain. OEMs are retreating from aggressive BEV-only timelines:

- Stellantis pivoting to PHEVs in Europe, conventional hybrids in North America
- BYD surpassed Tesla globally in 2024 (2.26M vs 1.6M EVs), with a 25%+ cost advantage
- Federal EV tax credits (30D, 25E, 45W) repealed for vehicles acquired after Sept 30, 2025
- OEM EBITDA margins declined from 11% (late 2024) to below 8% (late 2025)
- BEV growth CAGR: 9.6%

The transition is happening but the economics are punishing. Every OEM is running a dual powertrain portfolio, doubling platform complexity and R&D cost.

### 2. Software-Defined Vehicles (SDVs): The Defining Transformation Vector

Vehicles are increasingly defined by software capabilities rather than hardware specifications. This is the single most important trend for transformation consulting.

- Automotive software market: $31B (2018) to projected $80B (2030) per McKinsey
- Revenue opportunity: up to $1,600 per vehicle from connected offerings
- Margin improvement from SDV: up to $7,500/car (premium), $2,600/car (volume) per BCG
- Development cycles compressing: 48-60 months down to 24-36 months
- BMW Neue Klasse and Mercedes embedded AI-powered OS leading the way
- Stellantis moved 30%+ of R&D engineering from mechanical to embedded systems, cybersecurity, AI
- OTA (over-the-air) updates now table stakes

### 3. Direct-to-Consumer Model Evolution

- Franchised dealers still hold 77% of NA automotive market (2025)
- DTC models expanding at 8.7% annually
- Tesla's DTC model under legal challenge in multiple states
- Core tension: OEMs want data ownership and customer relationship; dealers own the last mile
- OEMs experimenting with online configuration/ordering while preserving dealer networks

### 4. Autonomous Driving: Concentrated, Not Distributed

L4 autonomy is commercially viable but concentrated in Waymo (3,000 robotaxis, 500K paid rides/week, $16B raise at $126B valuation). Most OEMs have retreated from in-house autonomous programs. Cruise shut down (2024). Ford/VW scaled back. The robotaxi market is separating from the private-ownership market.

### 5. Connected Car Services & Subscriptions

- Connected car market: $51.6B (2025)
- Automotive subscription services: $6.4B (2025), projected $12B by 2032 (9.3% CAGR)
- Consumer resistance to paying subscriptions for previously-included features (heated seats, etc.)
- Cloud/OTA infrastructure becoming strategic investment priority

## Forces

### Competitive Rivalry: VERY HIGH

Intense competition among GM, Toyota, Ford, Hyundai-Kia, Honda, Stellantis. High fixed costs create exit barriers. Low switching costs for buyers. Slow industry growth (flat to declining in 2026). EV transition creating new competitive dimensions (software, charging network, battery tech). Price competition intensifying as tariffs raise costs and consumers resist.

### Threat of New Entrants: MODERATE-HIGH (rising)

US tariffs are the primary barrier keeping Chinese EV makers out. BYD (5.2M total units) and peers have a decade of government-subsidized lead in battery tech, cost engineering, and production speed. If the tariff wall cracks (Mexico factories, trade deals), the competitive landscape transforms overnight. Tech entrants (Sony-Honda Afeela) remain low-threat due to manufacturing barriers. Rivian and Lucid are niche and capital-constrained.

### Threat of Substitutes: MODERATE (growing)

Ride-sharing (Uber/Lyft) reducing individual ownership in urban markets. Robotaxis (Waymo at 500K rides/week) could structurally reduce car ownership in cities. Remote work reducing commuting demand. Micro-mobility for short trips. Ownership still dominant in suburban/rural US, but urban substitution accelerating.

### Supplier Power: HIGH

Battery/cell manufacturers (CATL, LG, Panasonic, Samsung SDI) have very high power -- battery = 30-40% of EV cost. Semiconductor supply still constrained (20-52 week lead times for auto-grade). Software platform providers (Qualcomm, NVIDIA, Google AAOS) gaining leverage as OEMs depend on their platforms. Raw materials (lithium, cobalt, rare earth) geopolitically concentrated. Tariffs added 25% to imported steel/aluminum.

### Buyer Power: MODERATE-HIGH

Low switching costs between brands. High price transparency (online research, KBB, CarGurus). Affordability crisis from tariff-driven price increases. Franchise dealer model limits OEM control over customer experience. Fleet buyers have significant negotiating leverage.

## Regulatory Environment

### Federal: Deregulation Under Trump Administration (2025-2026)

| Action | Impact |
|--------|--------|
| EPA GHG Endangerment Finding rescinded (Feb 2026) | Removes legal foundation for all federal vehicle GHG standards |
| Biden-era Clean Car Standards repealed | 85 g/mile CO2 target for MY 2027-2032 eliminated |
| NHTSA CAFE rollback proposed | Reset to MY 2022 levels, +0.25-0.5%/year instead of 2%/year |
| IRA EV Tax Credits repealed (after Sept 30, 2025) | $7,500 new EV credit eliminated |
| 30C EV charger credit repealed (after June 30, 2026) | Reduces charging infrastructure investment incentive |
| California Clean Air Act waivers revoked | Blocks CA from setting stricter vehicle standards |

17 automakers publicly opposed the rollbacks. Ford broke ranks to praise them.

### State Level: Legal Fragmentation

California ACC II (100% ZEV by 2035) adopted by 11 states + DC. Federal CRA resolutions revoked California's waiver authority. Coalition of states filed lawsuit. Governor Newsom signed EO to maintain state enforcement. Net effect: regulatory fragmentation -- OEMs face a possible patchwork of state vs. federal requirements. Uncertainty is the dominant condition.

## Implications for Engagement

### What a Transformation Consultant Needs to Know That Isn't Obvious

**1. The operating model problem is harder than the technology problem.**

OEMs have no shortage of SDV vision decks. What they lack is the organizational architecture to execute. The core structural contradiction: software requires persistent teams with continuous delivery, but OEMs are organized around vehicle programs (projects with start/end dates). Every OEM that has created a standalone software unit (Cariad at VW, Stellantis Software) has struggled with the integration seam -- the software org builds capabilities the vehicle programs don't know how to consume. The consulting opportunity is not "teach them agile" -- it is redesigning the operating model to synchronize dual-track delivery (agile software + stage-gate hardware).

**2. Most OEMs are stuck in "agile theater."**

They have sprints, standups, and retrospectives at the team level, but the funding model is still CapEx project-based, the governance is still stage-gate, and the portfolio management is still annual planning. Agile practices without operating model transformation produces ritual without outcomes. The diagnostic question is not "are you doing agile?" but "can a team ship a software update to a vehicle in production without a vehicle program manager approving it?"

**3. The dual-powertrain era is a portfolio complexity bomb.**

Every OEM is simultaneously maintaining ICE, hybrid, PHEV, and BEV platforms. This 4x platform explosion is destroying engineering productivity and R&D budgets. The consulting angle is not powertrain strategy per se -- it is helping OEMs manage portfolio complexity through platform product management (shared platforms with product ownership, not per-vehicle-program engineering).

**4. Mid-tier OEMs face a proportionally harder transformation challenge.**

A company like Subaru (~644K units, ~4% share) cannot outspend GM or Toyota on software transformation. They need to get organizational change right because they cannot compensate with capital. This makes operating model consulting disproportionately valuable to mid-tier OEMs -- the cost of getting it wrong is existential, not merely expensive.

**5. Regulatory whiplash makes 5-year planning nearly impossible.**

Vehicle programs are 3-5 year cycles. Regulations are changing every 1-2 years. OEMs cannot commit to powertrain mix, emissions targets, or market strategy with any confidence. The consulting implication: help clients build adaptive planning capabilities (shorter commitment horizons, option-based portfolio strategy, scenario planning) rather than traditional strategic plans. The organizations that win are the ones that can change direction fastest, not the ones with the best 5-year plan.

**6. The talent gap is structural, not cyclical.**

OEMs cannot attract software talent at automotive pay scales -- they are competing with FAANG for the same engineers. Stellantis moved 30%+ of R&D staff from mechanical to embedded/software, but retraining mechanical engineers into software engineers has limits. The consulting opportunity: help OEMs design build-vs-buy strategies for software capabilities, architect their E/E platforms to minimize proprietary software surface area, and redesign compensation/culture to attract software talent.

**7. The real margin story is in connected services, not vehicle sales.**

Vehicle margins are compressing (EBITDA from 11% to below 8%). The growth margin opportunity is in connected services ($51.6B market, 9.3% CAGR), software feature unlocks (up to $1,600/vehicle), and data monetization. But OEMs are organizationally incapable of running subscription businesses -- they are engineered for one-time sales through dealer networks. Standing up product teams with P&L ownership for connected services is a specific, high-value consulting engagement pattern.

**8. Tariff exposure is reshoring production but not simplifying supply chains.**

US-built vehicles are up 4pp to >55% of sales, but even domestically built vehicles see $1,600-$2,000 cost increases from parts tariffs. The supply chain is not getting simpler -- it is getting more expensive everywhere. OEMs need supply chain resilience strategy (near-shoring, dual-sourcing, vertical integration of critical components) not just tariff mitigation.
