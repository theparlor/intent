# Persona: Team Lead in Transition

## Who

Scrum Master, tech lead, or platform manager within M.A.R.S. who is being coached through the Agile transformation. Archetypes include Gino Guarnere (Director, M.A.R.S. IT — has prior Scrum experience from Comcast), Marc Rullo (SubaruNet team manager), and Kadeem Williams (TechShare). There are approximately 6 team leads across the in-scope teams (Subaru.net, TechShare, Subaru.com, M.A.R.S./Martech, QA, DevOps).

These leads sit at the most uncomfortable position in the transformation: they're accountable for team output AND for adopting new ways of working. They didn't choose the transformation — it was catalyzed by the CIO and sponsored by Greg — but they're the ones who have to make it real in daily standups, sprint planning, and backlog refinement.

Maturity varies significantly. Gino brings Comcast Scrum experience; others may have never run a retrospective. The engagement's training curriculum (13 topics from Agile Mindset through Planning for Adaptability) flows through these leads — they absorb it, model it, and translate it for their teams.

## Behaviors

- Protective of their team's time. Every training session or coaching hour competes with sprint commitments. They'll attend, but they're mentally calculating the cost.
- Adopt selectively. They'll embrace ceremonies that solve real problems (e.g., standups for visibility) and resist ones that feel performative (e.g., retrospectives when there's no safe space to raise real issues).
- Watch their peers. If Gino's team adopts and sees benefit, other leads follow. If one lead visibly struggles, others hedge. The peer dynamic across the 6 teams is a powerful adoption vector.
- Translate between consultant-speak and team-speak. Turnberry uses frameworks and methodology language; the teams use "what do I need to do by Friday." The lead bridges this gap.
- Default to JIRA as the scoreboard. Whatever happens in ceremonies, the real question is "what does JIRA say?" Leads who feel JIRA is being imposed will resist by under-maintaining it; leads who feel JIRA gives them leverage will become power users.
- Some already have informal cadences that work. The transformation standardization feels like it's replacing something functional with something mandated. This creates a "why are we fixing what isn't broken" response.

## Needs & Pain Points

- PP-001: Being asked to adopt Scrum ceremonies and Agile artifacts while still delivering on existing commitments — no slack in the schedule for learning
- PP-002: JIRA migration threatens the team's muscle memory — they have workflows that work, even if they're inconsistent with the org standard
- PP-003: Role ambiguity between Scrum Master, tech lead, and team manager — the engagement adds new responsibilities without removing old ones
- PP-004: Ceremony fatigue — training sessions, coaching sessions, PI Planning prep, office hours, all on top of the actual work
- PP-005: Variable team maturity means some leads are coaching basics while others are ready for advanced patterns — one-size training feels wrong for both ends

## What They Need from the Engagement

- **A clear Roles & Responsibilities framework** — The R&R Workbook deliverable directly addresses PP-003. But it needs to be practical, not aspirational. "Here's what YOU do, here's what the Scrum Master does, here's what the PO does" in concrete terms.
- **JIRA that helps, not JIRA that reports** — If the standardized JIRA setup gives them better visibility into their own team's work (not just a dashboard for Greg), they'll adopt. If it feels like surveillance, they'll game the metrics.
- **Coaching that meets them where they are** — Gino doesn't need Scrum 101. A SubaruNet lead who's never run a sprint does. The Monday Agile Office Hours (3-4pm) format works only if the content adapts to the audience in the room.
- **Permission to keep what works** — Not every team needs to abandon their current JIRA setup wholesale. Acknowledging existing functional patterns reduces resistance.

## Open Questions

- Which leads have prior Agile experience beyond Gino? This significantly affects coaching approach per team.
- Are any leads actively resistant (vs. passively skeptical)? Resistance patterns differ and require different interventions.
- Do leads have authority to modify JIRA configuration for their teams, or is that centrally controlled? This affects their sense of ownership.
- How do leads feel about the "tech PO" model? Do they want business partner involvement or prefer the current autonomy?
- What is the support model that competes for team time? (Documented as a discovery question but answer unclear.)
