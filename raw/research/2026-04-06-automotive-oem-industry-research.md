- **2026:** Projected decline to 15.9M (-3%). Tariff hangover, credit expiration, price inflation dampening demand. January SAAR already signaling weakness.
- **Structural headwinds:** Average transaction prices up 10%+, affordability crisis emerging, used car market absorbing more demand.

## 2. Major Trends

### EV Transition

| Metric | Value |
|--------|-------|
| Global EV sales (2025) | 18M units (up from 14M in 2024) |
| US ICE powertrain share | 82% (2025) |
| BEV growth CAGR | 9.6% |
| BYD surpassed Tesla | 2024 -- 2.26M EVs vs Tesla's 1.6M |
| Chinese EV price advantage | 25%+ lower cost; BYD Seagull at <$10K |

**Strategic reality:** The EV transition is real but non-linear. OEMs are retreating from aggressive BEV-only timelines:
- Stellantis pivoting to PHEVs in Europe, conventional hybrids in North America
- China has moved from policy-driven to market-driven EV adoption; US/Europe still depend on incentives
- Federal EV tax credits (30D, 25E, 45W) repealed for vehicles acquired after Sept 30, 2025
- OEM profitability under pressure: EBITDA margins declined from 11% (late 2024) to below 8% (late 2025)

### Software-Defined Vehicles (SDVs)

The defining transformation vector for the industry. Vehicles increasingly defined by software capabilities rather than hardware specifications.

**Key developments:**
- BMW Neue Klasse SDV platform launched
- Mercedes-Benz embedded AI-powered OS with productivity features
- OTA (over-the-air) updates now table stakes -- enable continuous feature delivery post-sale
- Development cycles compressing: 48-60 months down to 24-36 months
- Automotive software market: $31B (2018) to projected $80B (2030) per McKinsey
- Revenue opportunity: up to $1,600 per vehicle from connected offerings
- Margin improvement from SDV: up to $7,500/car premium segment, $2,600/car volume segment (BCG)
- R&D reorientation: Stellantis moved 30%+ of R&D engineering from mechanical to embedded systems, cybersecurity, AI

### Direct-to-Consumer Models

- Franchised dealers still hold 77% of NA automotive market (2025)
- DTC models expanding at 8.7% annually
- Tesla's DTC model under legal challenge in multiple states
- Rivian operating company-owned showrooms
- OEMs experimenting with online configuration/ordering while preserving dealer networks
- Tension: OEMs want data ownership and customer relationship; dealers own the last mile

### Autonomous Driving

| Player | Status |
|--------|--------|
| Waymo | Dominant. 3,000 robotaxis, 500K paid rides/week, 4M rider-only miles/week. Operating in Phoenix, LA, SF, Austin. Expanding to Atlanta, Miami, DC, Boston, Philly. $16B raise at $126B valuation (Feb 2026) |
| Cruise | Shut down (2024) |
| Nuro/Uber | 20,000 Gravity SUVs planned for H2 2026 over 6 years |
| Ford/VW | Scaled back self-driving plans (2022-2024) |
| Tesla FSD | L2+ supervised, not true L4. Regulatory uncertainty |

**Bottom line:** L4 autonomy is commercially viable but concentrated in Waymo. Most OEMs have retreated from in-house autonomous programs. The robotaxi market is separating from the private-ownership market.

### Connected Car Services & Subscriptions

- Connected car market: $51.6B (2025), growing rapidly
- Automotive subscription services: $6.4B (2025), projected $12B by 2032 (9.3% CAGR)
- OEMs pursuing recurring revenue via feature unlocks, insurance partnerships, data monetization
- Cloud/OTA infrastructure becoming strategic investment priority
- Consumer resistance to paying subscriptions for previously-included features (heated seats, etc.)

## 3. Technology Transformation: The Hardware-to-Software Pivot

### Organizational Restructuring

OEMs are undergoing the most significant organizational transformation since the assembly line:

1. **Dedicated software units:** Most major OEMs have created standalone software organizations (e.g., Cariad at VW, Stellantis Software, BMW iX software team)
2. **Agile adoption:** Cross-functional teams, continuous release cadence, outcome-based objectives replacing waterfall hardware-development processes
3. **Talent reorientation:** Stellantis moved 30%+ of R&D staff from mechanical to embedded/software. Industry-wide shortage of automotive software engineers
4. **Development cycle compression:** From 48-60 month to 24-36 month vehicle programs
5. **E/E architecture modernization:** Zonal architectures replacing distributed ECUs -- simplifying wiring, reducing BOM, consolidating compute

### Agile/Product Operating Model Adoption

This is where transformation consulting has its highest leverage:

- **The core tension:** Software development requires agile cadence (2-week sprints, continuous delivery). Hardware development is waterfall by physics (tooling, testing, homologation). OEMs must synchronize both.
- **VW's approach:** Systematic introduction of "agile product organization" -- IT and Board functions in cross-functional teams with short development cycles
- **Industry pattern:** Most OEMs are in "agile theater" stage -- agile practices at team level without operating model transformation at org level
- **GenAI acceleration:** AI is shortening R&D cycles through faster design iteration and code generation, but creating new skill gaps

### Financial Inflection

After years of heavy investment in SDV platforms, autonomous driving, and electrification, OEMs hit a financial inflection point in 2025:
- EBITDA margins compressed from 11% to below 8%
- Tariff costs: $35.4B+ across industry since 2025
- Volvo cut 3,000 jobs (May 2025) citing cost pressure
- Tension between investment in software future and shareholder returns from hardware present

## 4. Porter's Five Forces Analysis

### Competitive Rivalry: VERY HIGH

- Intense competition among GM, Toyota, Ford, Hyundai-Kia, Honda, Stellantis
- High fixed costs create exit barriers
- Low switching costs for buyers
- Slow industry growth (flat to declining in 2026)
- EV transition creating new competitive dimensions (software, charging network, battery tech)
- Price competition intensifying as tariffs raise costs and consumers resist

### Threat of New Entrants: MODERATE-HIGH (and rising)

| Entrant | Threat Level | Notes |
|---------|-------------|-------|
| Tesla | Established | Declining share but still category-defining. DTC model, software-first |
| Chinese OEMs (BYD, Geely, NIO, XPeng) | HIGH but blocked by tariffs | 25%+ cost advantage. BYD at 5.2M units. Currently blocked by US tariffs but exploring Mexico manufacturing |
| Rivian | Moderate | Cash-constrained, niche luxury adventure segment |
| Lucid | Low | Low volume, Saudi backing, luxury niche |
| Tech companies (Apple abandoned, Sony-Honda Afeela) | Low-Moderate | Capital and manufacturing barriers remain high |

**Key dynamic:** US tariffs are the primary barrier keeping Chinese EV makers out. If that wall cracks (Mexico factories, trade deals), the competitive landscape transforms overnight. Chinese OEMs have a decade of government-subsidized lead in battery tech, cost engineering, and production speed.

### Threat of Substitutes: MODERATE (and growing)

- Ride-sharing (Uber/Lyft) reducing individual ownership in urban markets
- Robotaxis (Waymo) -- 500K rides/week and scaling. Could structurally reduce car ownership in cities
- Public transit investment (varies by metro)
- Remote work reducing commuting demand
- Micro-mobility (e-bikes, scooters) for short trips
- **Net effect:** Ownership still dominant in suburban/rural US, but urban substitution accelerating

### Supplier Power: HIGH

| Supplier Category | Power Level | Notes |
|-------------------|------------|-------|
| Battery/cell manufacturers | Very High | Concentrated supply (CATL, LG, Panasonic, Samsung SDI). Battery = 30-40% of EV cost |
| Semiconductor (chips) | High | Post-2021 shortage revealed dependency. TSMC, Samsung, Intel. Lead times still 20-52 weeks for auto-grade |
| Software/tech platforms | Rising | Qualcomm (Snapdragon Ride), NVIDIA (DRIVE), Google (AAOS) gaining leverage as OEMs depend on their platforms |
| Raw materials (lithium, cobalt, rare earth) | High | Geopolitically concentrated (China, DRC, Chile). IRA domestic sourcing requirements add cost |
| Steel/aluminum | Moderate-High | Tariffs added 25% to imported steel/aluminum costs |

### Buyer Power: MODERATE-HIGH

- Low switching costs between brands
- High price transparency (online research, KBB, CarGurus)
- Affordability crisis: average transaction price up 10%+ due to tariffs
- Franchise dealer model limits OEM control over customer experience
- Growing buyer sophistication around TCO (total cost of ownership) for EVs vs ICE
- Fleet buyers (rental, corporate) have significant negotiating leverage

## 5. Regulatory Environment

### Federal: Deregulation Under Trump Administration (2025-2026)

| Action | Status | Impact |
|--------|--------|--------|
| EPA GHG Endangerment Finding (2009) | Rescinded (Feb 2026) | Removes legal foundation for all federal vehicle GHG standards |
| Biden-era Clean Car Standards (MY 2027-2032) | Repealed | 85 g/mile CO2 target eliminated |
| NHTSA CAFE rollback | Proposed (comment period ended Jan 2026) | Reset to MY 2022 levels, +0.25-0.5%/year instead of 2%/year |
| IRA EV Tax Credits (30D, 25E, 45W) | Repealed for vehicles after Sept 30, 2025 | $7,500 new EV credit eliminated |
| 30C EV charger credit | Repealed for chargers after June 30, 2026 | Reduces charging infrastructure investment incentive |
| California Clean Air Act waivers | Revoked via CRA (June 2025) | Blocks CA from setting stricter vehicle standards |

**Industry response:** 17 automakers publicly called on Trump administration to reverse course, warning of "extended period of litigation and instability." Ford notably broke ranks to praise the rollback as aligning with "customer choice."

### State Level: Legal Battle

- California ACC II (adopted 2022): 35% ZEV by MY 2026, 68% by 2030, 100% by 2035
- 11 states + DC adopted California's standards
- Federal CRA resolutions revoked California's waiver authority
- Coalition of states filed lawsuit against federal government
- Governor Newsom signed EO N-27-25 (June 2025) to maintain state enforcement
- **Net effect:** Regulatory fragmentation. OEMs face possible patchwork of state vs. federal requirements, or potential return to a single (weaker) federal standard. Uncertainty is the dominant condition.

### Tariffs (Active)

| Tariff | Rate | Impact |
|--------|------|--------|
| Imported vehicles | 25% | Price hikes of $5,000-$8,900 per vehicle |
| Auto parts | 25% | Even domestically built vehicles see $1,600-$2,000 cost increase |
| Steel | 25% | Across all steel imports |
| Aluminum | 25% | Across all aluminum imports |
| **Total industry cost** | **$35.4B+ since 2025** | Toyota hardest hit ($9.1B). Detroit Big 3 collectively $6.5B in 2025 |

US-built vehicles now >55% of sales (up 4pp YoY) as tariffs reshape production geography.

## 6. Consulting Implications

### Where Transformation Consultants Add Value

Based on the industry dynamics above, these are the highest-leverage consulting engagement types:

#### Tier 1: Operating Model Transformation (Highest Value)

1. **Hardware-to-software operating model redesign** -- Helping OEMs restructure from waterfall hardware orgs to dual-track (agile software + stage-gate hardware) operating models. This is the single biggest organizational challenge in the industry.

2. **Product operating model adoption** -- Moving from project-based to product-based organization. Persistent teams, outcome-based objectives, product ownership vs. program management. Most OEMs are in early/struggling stages.

3. **Agile at scale** -- Beyond team-level agile to enterprise agile. Portfolio management, funding model changes (CapEx to OpEx), value stream mapping, PI planning for automotive context.

#### Tier 2: Strategy & Positioning

4. **EV/electrification strategy** -- Portfolio decisions (BEV vs PHEV vs hybrid), timing, market-by-market strategy given regulatory fragmentation
5. **Software monetization strategy** -- Subscription models, feature unlocks, data monetization, connected services pricing
6. **Supply chain resilience** -- Semiconductor strategy, battery sourcing, tariff mitigation, near-shoring decisions
7. **DTC vs dealer model evolution** -- Customer experience redesign, data ownership, omnichannel strategy

#### Tier 3: Execution & Capability Building

8. **Software engineering capability** -- Talent strategy, build vs buy, tech stack decisions, DevOps/CI-CD for automotive, cybersecurity
9. **E/E architecture modernization** -- Zonal architecture migration, BOM simplification, compute consolidation
10. **Regulatory compliance navigation** -- Managing across federal deregulation, state mandates, and international requirements simultaneously

### Common OEM Pain Points

| Pain Point | Root Cause |
|-----------|-----------|
| "Agile theater" -- rituals without outcomes | Operating model not changed; teams do sprints but org runs waterfall |
| Software quality at launch | Automotive safety culture clashes with move-fast software culture |
| Talent gap | Can't attract software talent at automotive pay scales; competing with FAANG |
| Portfolio complexity explosion | Too many platforms, powertrains (ICE, hybrid, PHEV, BEV), and SKUs |
| Siloed digital teams | Multiple groups building similar features without end-to-end service delivery process |
| Supplier dependency | Critical IP (chips, batteries, software platforms) controlled by non-automotive suppliers |
| Regulatory whiplash | Can't plan 5-year vehicle programs when rules change every 2 years |
| Margin compression | Heavy R&D investment in software/EV future while hardware present generates the cash |

### Where Product Operating Models Fit

Product operating models address the core structural problem: **OEMs are organized around vehicle programs (projects with start/end dates) but software requires persistent teams with continuous delivery.**

Specific application areas:
- **Connected services teams:** Should be product teams with P&L ownership, not project teams reporting to vehicle programs
- **Digital customer experience:** App, configurator, owner portal -- all need product management, not project management
- **Platform teams:** Shared software platforms (infotainment, ADAS, connectivity) need platform product management
- **Internal tools/data:** Engineering tools, data platforms, analytics -- the "internal products" that enable the rest

**The Subaru-specific angle:** SOA is a mid-tier OEM (~644K units, ~4% share) without the resources to build everything in-house. Their transformation challenge is proportionally harder -- they need to adopt product operating models while being capital-constrained relative to GM/Toyota/VW. This makes the consulting engagement around operating model change highly relevant: they need to get organizational transformation right because they can't outspend the problem.
