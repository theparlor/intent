## Summary

Strong, intellectually honest brief that passes most rigor tests. The problem statement nails root cause, the opportunity/solution separation is clean, and the Cagan alignment is honest. Key gaps in competitive realism, audience concreteness, and GTM credibility.

## Scores

| Criterion | Score | Key Finding |
|-----------|-------|-------------|
| Problem clarity | 4/5 | Nails root cause but conflates Ceremony Tax and Specification Gap as distinct pains |
| Audience specificity | 3/5 | Behavioral personas but not concrete enough for LinkedIn test |
| Opportunity vs solution | 4.5/5 | Clean separation; honest about Cagan alignment |
| Hypothesis rigor | 3.5/5 | Falsifiable but missing null hypotheses and operationalized metrics |
| Competitive honesty | 2.5/5 | Asserts uniqueness at intersection but doesn't prove teams want a new OS vs better execution of existing one |
| GTM realism | 2/5 | Optimistic for solo practitioner; needs staffing/capital reality check |

## Critical Questions

1. **Do teams want a new operating model or better execution of their current one?** This is the existential question. A fake-door test ("post-Agile OS" vs "better spec quality within Scrum") would answer it.

2. **Can Brien sustain thought leadership + community + methodology development as a solo practitioner?** The GTM timeline reads like a plan for 3-5 people.

3. **What are teams willing to pay for?** Playbook? Workshops? Tooling? The brief doesn't pick a monetization path.

4. **How do you operationalize "spec quality"?** It's mentioned repeatedly but never defined measurably.

## Highest-Leverage Next Step

**5 in-depth interviews with teams actively struggling with AI + Agile friction.** Validate whether the Ceremony Tax is really felt, whether it's the main pain, and whether a new operating model (vs. better execution of the old one) is what they'd adopt.
